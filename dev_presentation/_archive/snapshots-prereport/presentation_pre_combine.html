Project 13 — Text to SpeechRoadmap

What is VITS?

One network, text → raw waveform — no separate vocoder, no intermediate mel. A conditional VAE with a normalizing-flow prior, an adversarial (GAN) decoder, and a stochastic duration predictor. (Kim, Kong & Son, ICML 2021)

ComponentRoleTrainInfer
Text encodertext → prior over z✓✓
Posterior encoderreal-audio spectrogram → posterior z✓—
Flowinvertible map z ↔ z_p✓✓
Duration predictorlength of each token✓✓
HiFi-GAN decoderz → waveform✓✓
Train vs. inference: the posterior sees real audio (train only); the text-prior learns to match it (KL). At inference there's no audio → sample z from the prior.
[DIMA] What is VITS? One network from text straight to a raw waveform — no separate vocoder, no intermediate spectrogram (that seam is where two-stage systems lose quality). Under the hood it's a conditional VAE with a normalizing-flow prior, an adversarial GAN decoder, and a stochastic duration predictor. Here are the pieces — and the key subtlety is this column: the posterior encoder only exists in training. It reads the real audio to make a high-quality latent; the text prior is trained to match it — that's the KL term. At inference there's no audio, so we sample the latent from the prior and decode. That asymmetry is the heart of VITS — and it creates one hard problem.
Project 13 — Text to SpeechThe model

The alignment problem → MAS

Text tokens and audio frames have different lengths and no ground-truth alignment.

Monotonic Alignment Search (MAS): a dynamic-programming search (Viterbi-like) for the highest-likelihood monotonic text→frame map.

tokens → H  E  L  L  O
frames → HH EEE L LLLL OOOOO   ← monotonic, never goes backward

We implemented MAS from scratch: a vectorized forward pass + backward traceback. It guarantees a valid alignment — unlike attention, it can't skip or babble.

[DIMA] The problem is alignment. We have a few text tokens and hundreds of audio frames, and no labels saying which frames belong to which letter. VITS solves it with Monotonic Alignment Search — a dynamic-programming search, like Viterbi, for the best monotonic map from text to frames. Monotonic means speech moves left to right, never jumping back. We implemented MAS from scratch: a vectorized forward pass then a backward traceback. It guarantees a valid alignment by construction, so unlike attention it can't skip or babble.

[DIMA] Here's the core engineering challenge. Hugging Face ships VITS as inference-only — no training code at all: no forward pass, no alignment, no losses, no discriminator. forward_train — the training pass

For each batch:

  1. Text encoder → prior mean, log-var
  2. Posterior encoder (real spectrogram) → latent z
  3. Flow z → z_p
  4. MAS → monotonic text→frame alignment
  5. Duration loss vs. MAS-derived durations
  6. Random segment slice of z → decoder → predicted waveform
    (decoder is costly → train on a short ~0.74 s crop, not the whole clip)
  7. Return waveform + mel, prior/posterior params → feed the losses
[DIMA] This is one training step. We encode the text into a prior. The posterior encoder turns the real audio into a latent code. The flow transforms it. MAS aligns text to frames, giving a duration loss. Then the important trick — step six. The decoder that generates the waveform is expensive, so instead of decoding the whole clip we take a short random slice, about three-quarters of a second, decode just that, and compare against the matching slice of real audio. That's from HiFi-GAN, and because the decoder is convolutional, a short crop trains it fine while saving huge memory.

The loss = five terms

TermWhatWeight
ReconstructionL1 on the mel (predicted vs. target)45
KLposterior ‖ prior on the latent (the VAE term)1
Durationfrom the stochastic duration predictor1
Adversarialleast-squares GAN (LSGAN) for G and D1
Feature matchingL1 on the discriminator's feature maps1

Reconstruction is weighted 45× — spectrum accuracy matters most; the adversarial + feature-matching terms add realism and stabilize the GAN.

[DIMA] All of that feeds five loss terms. Reconstruction compares the generated spectrum to the real one — and we weight it 45 times higher than the rest, because getting the spectrum right matters most. KL ties the text prior to the audio posterior — the VAE part. Duration trains the rhythm. And the adversarial and feature-matching terms come from the GAN — they add realism and, just as importantly, keep training stable.

Discriminator + the GAN loop

HiFi-GAN discriminators, from scratch: MPD — audio folded by prime periods (2,3,5,7,11) → pitch & harmonics; MSD — 3 time scales. Both expose feature maps → feature-matching loss.

  • D step — real vs. detached fake → discriminator loss
  • G step — recon + KL + duration + adversarial + feature-matching
  • two AdamW · batch 8 · gen lr 2e-5 · disc lr 2e-4 · resumable _G/_D.pt · Colab L4
Decisive, not optional: our first loop had only reconstruction loss (no discriminator) and produced pure noise — Whisper WER 90–120%. The discriminator is the missing half of the VITS objective: HF ships the generator, not the adversary.
[DIMA] Those adversarial losses need a discriminator — built from scratch, HiFi-GAN style: a Multi-Period Discriminator that folds the audio by short prime periods, 2 through 11, to catch pitch and harmonics, and a Multi-Scale Discriminator across three time scales; both expose feature maps for the feature-matching loss. The loop is a standard GAN alternation: each step the discriminator trains first on real versus detached fake, then the generator trains on all five losses to fool it. Two AdamW optimizers, batch 8, generator learning rate 2e-5 and discriminator 2e-4, resumable checkpoints, on a Colab L4. And this mattered: our very first loop had only the reconstruction objective — no discriminator — and it generated pure noise, Whisper word error rate 90 to 120 percent. The discriminator is the missing half of the VITS objective; Hugging Face ships the generator but not the adversary. But getting it to actually speak took one more fight.

Fine-tuning broke — then taught us VITS

The trainer ran (losses fell), but the first fine-tune spoke too fast and unintelligibly. We debugged by the inference WER — not the loss curve:

SymptomChangeResult
1 s output, WER 110%first GAN run, lr 2e-4broken
duration loss oscillatinglr 2e-4 → 2e-5 (10× too high → catastrophic forgetting)2 s, WER 90%
still 2 s, WER ~100%froze the duration predictorno change — a clue
traced the inference pathits input — the text encoder — still drifted via KL → froze it toofixed ✅
Root cause: for a timbre-only fine-tune, train only the audio side (posterior · flow · decoder) and freeze the text/timing front-end — or it drifts out of what the duration predictor expects and durations collapse.

Lesson: a falling reconstruction loss says nothing about generation — the stochastic duration loss is inherently noisy, so only the inference WER exposed every bug.

[DIMA] Here's the honest part — and the part that taught us the most. Our first fine-tune trained cleanly, the losses fell — but when we generated speech it came out one second long and unintelligible, word error rate over 100 percent. We debugged it by listening and by measuring Whisper WER, not by trusting the loss curve. Step one: the learning rate, 2e-4, was the from-scratch value — ten times too high for fine-tuning, so it was catastrophically forgetting the pretrained voice. Dropping it to 2e-5 helped but didn't fix it. Step two: we froze the duration predictor — and nothing changed, which was the real clue. Step three: we traced the actual inference code and saw that the duration predictor reads the text encoder, which was STILL training via the KL term and drifting. Once we froze the whole text-and-timing front-end and trained only the audio side — posterior, flow, decoder — it worked. The lesson: for a timbre-only change you must freeze the front-end, and a falling reconstruction loss tells you nothing about generation quality — only inference WER does. Ilya, how did we score it?

Evaluation

Objective, ASR-based intelligibility (lower = better):

  • WER / CER — intelligibility. Whisper (independent, frozen ASR) transcribes our audio → compare to the prompt. "Can a listener understand it?"
  • MCD — spectral fidelity vs. real audio. Meaningful for matching a voice — but not for a droid: it'd compare against the original female LJSpeech recording, a different timbre on purpose.

Before/after = one flag. --checkpoint pretrained vs. the fine-tuned _G.pt, same prompts both times → apples-to-apples. Held out the last 500 LJSpeech clips (no leakage).

[ILYA] We evaluate intelligibility objectively. We run Whisper — an independent, frozen, off-the-shelf ASR that never touches training — on our generated audio and compare its transcript to the prompt: word and character error rate, lower is better. Can a listener understand it? We also have MCD, spectral distortion against the real recording — but it's only meaningful when you're trying to MATCH a voice. For a droid it would compare us against the original female LJSpeech audio, a deliberately different timbre, so a high MCD wouldn't mean we failed — it's the wrong target here. So WER and CER are our objective measure. The comparison is clean: one checkpoint flag, the same prompts both times, and we held out the last 500 LJSpeech clips so there's no leakage. Project 13 — Text to SpeechStatus

Failure modes (and risks)

  • Mispronunciation — rare words, names, numbers, via grapheme→phoneme errors (the model already uses an espeak phoneme tokenizer).
  • Prosody — flat/odd rhythm; stochastic duration helps, isn't perfect.
  • Artifacts — metallic/buzzing GAN artifacts, esp. early in training.
  • Fine-tuning risks — too-high LR wrecks pretrained weights · GAN instability · over-fitting the B1 timbre.
  • Timbre ≠ effect — RVC transfers a speaker's timbre, not the ring-modulator "robot" character, so the result is a brighter, thinner voice rather than a hard droid. The mechanical buzz lives in a separate DSP effect, not the training data.
[ILYA] Briefly, the failure modes. Mispronunciation of rare words and names — the model already uses an espeak phoneme tokenizer, so these are grapheme-to-phoneme errors, not a missing-phonemes problem. Prosody — rhythm can be flat. Artifacts — GANs can buzz, especially early. Fine-tuning risks — too high a learning rate wrecks the pretrained weights. And the honest one we verified by synthesizing with our checkpoint: fine-tuning DID shift the voice toward the target — brighter, thinner — but RVC transfers a speaker's TIMBRE, not the ring-modulator robot effect, and the pitch stays human. So it's a brighter voice, not a hard battle droid. That mechanical buzz is a separate DSP effect, never in the training data — to get a harder droid we'd bake that effect into the dataset or post-process the output.
